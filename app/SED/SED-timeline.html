  • July 2013

    • Kentucky

      July 22–24, 2013

      The Kentucky Substance Abuse Treatment Enhancement and Dissemination (KAT-ED) has a solid foundation in child and adolescent services and an active State Interagency Council for Services to Children with Emotional Disabilities (SIAC). The team has partnered with the Kentucky Partnership for Families and Children and has integrated youth and families across all activities. Youth are included in the SIAC and in program activities. The team has begun incorporating workforce survey information into a workforce development plan and map. Staff are being trained to implement evidence-based practices (EBPs), though a statewide plan for EBP implementation is yet to be developed. Kentucky is redesigning its children and adolescent services and systems. The KAT-ED team is involved in these discussions and will continue to address system changes as service implementation continues.

  • August 2013

    • Maine

      August 20–22, 2013

      The Maine Youth Treatment Recovery Enhancement Project (MYTREP) team has dedicated staff and partners who have laid the foundation to address statewide infrastructure. The project coordinator is committed to the project’s success, and the adolescent treatment coordinator has worked beyond the allocated time to support the project. There is active communication among the Office of Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services (SAMHS) management and SAT-ED staff and partners. Going forward, Maine can incorporate the SAT-ED initiative as an agency-wide priority and ensure the SAT-ED coordinator has direct access to high-level leadership within both SAMHSA and other child-serving agencies.

  • September 2013

    • Indiana

      September 10–12, 2013

      The Indiana SAT-ED’s principal investigator and project administrator have led in the initiative’s rapid advancement. There is active communication among management staff from the Indiana Division of Mental Health and Addiction (DMHA), SAT-ED staff, treatment providers, families, youth, and stakeholders. The initiative exhibits many strengths despite staffing delays and DMHA’s decision (approved by SAMHSA) to replace its assessment tool and treatment model. Indiana can use this momentum to build and sustain collaborative relationships across all child-serving agencies, complete financial maps, develop and execute a workforce training implementation plan, support the two evidence-based treatment provider sites, incorporate ideas suggested by youth and family members, and develop infrastructure evaluation measures.

    • New York

      September 24–26, 2013

      The New York Serving Adolescents in Need of Treatment (NY-SAINT) initiative is operational at the statewide and local site levels. The Office of Alcoholism and Substance Abuse Services (OASAS) leadership is dedicated and can use its staff experience and priority focus to increase support for NY-SAINT to accomplish numerous adolescent infrastructure tasks: identify a lead person for a workforce development plan and a chair for its financial mapping workgroup; organize the voice for families and youth to strengthen adolescent access to treatment; develop a strong and informed qualitative evaluation; focus on project-level outcomes with detailed timelines; and consider hiring individuals representative of its adolescent treatment population for any enhanced NY-SAINT staffing pattern.

  • October 2013

    • Montana

      October 29–31, 2013

      The Children’s Mental Health Bureau (CMHB) has a strong start to its SAT-ED implementation. Its partnership with the Addictive and Mental Disorders Division has provided a foundation for improving treatment for adolescents with substance use disorders and co-occurring substance use and mental health disorders. CMHB can further strengthen Montana’s adolescent treatment system and the project’s sustainability in several ways: augment project staff with an individual experienced in youth substance abuse issues; formalize memorandums of agreement encompassing financial mapping and workforce development with other agencies; continue developing the financial mapping workgroup and leadership; explore Medicaid match equality for services for youth with primary SUDs; increase the youth and family voice; and implement and disseminate an appropriate evidence-based practice.

  • November 2013

    • Oklahoma

      November 19–21, 2013

      The Oklahoma Department of Mental Health and Substance Abuse Services (ODMHSAS) has demonstrated a strong start to implementing its SAT-ED initiative. It has supported several training events for providers and other child-serving staff, such as the neuroscience of adolescent drug use and training for youth on leadership and peer recovery support. ODMHSAS can build on this momentum by augmenting SAT-ED staff, continuing to develop interagency collaboration, assembling a financial mapping workgroup, preparing a workforce dissemination plan, and increasing treatment access for youth with primary SUDs and their families.

  • December 2013

    • South Carolina

      December 10–12, 2013

      The Department of Alcohol and Other Drug Abuse Services (DAODAS) is strongly committed to its Collaboration for Success initiative. As a past recipient of a Co-Occurring State Incentive Grant and the State Adolescent Substance Abuse Treatment Coordination (SAC) grant, it can leverage State agency resources and strengthen system infrastructures. It would be beneficial to: recruit youth in recovery for the State’s Joint Council; develop a finance subcommittee; assign staff to focus on youth and family involvement; add a co-director with specific substance use disorder (SUD) knowledge to South Carolina’s Federation of Families; continue developing outreach activities; and integrate youth with SUDs into the Youth Move model.

  • April 2014

    • Iowa

      April 9–11, 2014

      Iowa’s strength is a strong funding mix that increases the sustainability potential for SAT-ED services. Iowa’s Department of Public Health (IDPH) has the opportunity to apply lessons learned from its midpoint implementation experience to the selection and placement of its imminently vacant position for SAT-ED director. The new director will address the financing and policy reforms, interagency collaboration, workforce development, and inclusion of the youth and family voice needed to sustain effective adolescent substance use disorder treatment.

    • Washington

      April 22–24, 2014

      Washington’s Division of Behavioral Health and Recovery (DBHR) has made much progress with its SAT-ED initiative and can build upon many strengths. The experienced project director and committed SAT-ED providers have histories of implementing evidence-based practices and attaining Government Performance and Result Act targets. DBHR has made significant progress on its first financial map. The initiative would benefit if its Family, Youth and System Partner Round Table (FYSPRT) council would embrace and address adolescent substance use disorder and develop formal and regular communication with high-level government policymakers. DBHR will work closely with SAMHSA to incorporate the Global Appraisal of Individual Needs assessment at additional provider sites in years 2 and 3.

  • May 2014

    • Louisiana

      May 6–8, 2014

      The Office of Behavioral Health (OBH) has made much progress with its SAT-ED initiative and can build upon many strengths. The initiative has a proactive approach developing a comprehensive array of services for youth with substance use disorders (SUD) and their families. It has progressed in developing a financial map, includes providers trained in evidence-based practices, has a competent evaluation team, and has developed a comprehensive site-level evaluation plan. OBH may want to augment SAT-ED staffing, infuse youth SUD issues into its interagency council workings, complete the baseline financial map, develop the workforce dissemination plan, strengthen pathways to youth SUD treatment, and develop a plan to evaluate infrastructure activities.

    • Massachusetts

      May 20–23, 2014

      The Bureau of Substance Abuse Services (BSAS) has made significant progress with its SAT-ED initiative and has numerous strengths to build upon. Its strong executive leadership and well-established interagency workgroup (IWG) focus on adolescent substance-related needs within the Commonwealth. BSAS has engaged two SAT-ED pilot providers with strong EBP implementation experience and valued history in their respective communities. This important initiative will be more likely to become institutionalized and sustained if the IWG gets representation from Medicaid and family advocates and if it establishes a formal financial mapping subcommittee.
